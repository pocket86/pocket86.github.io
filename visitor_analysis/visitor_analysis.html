Website: www.typeform.com
1) What are their demographics? Age, Income, Education, Geographic Location.

Technically, the typeform service could be utilized by anyone, however the site is promoted as a business solution. This being the case, they are targeting an audience of men and women in the work field, ranging in age anywhere from 18 to 65. Education is most likely a college 4-year degree in something relating to business or communications. Geographic location could be anywhere, as this service is an online tool.


2) How did the site meet the needs of the visitor (be specific in terms of "Less is More)?

This site does a good job at presenting information about the capabilities of the product. Although there is a lot of information to present, the mobile site does so in a clean, organized way that makes the site easy to navigate and understand.


3) Describe how the content was organized to facilitate it being found?

The mobile site makes great use of a slideing side-navigation panel. There is an obvious indicator to press to expand the panel, and listed on it are very logially-ordered links to different parts of the site, including "Home", "Examples", "Getting Started", etc.


4) What did the site do to make the mobile experience enjoyable (or at least not painful)?

In my opinion, the site was very enjoyable due to it's clean, uncluttered layout, the logical layout (following an order of 'what is it, what is it for, how do I use it, etc), and easy navigation. The size of links and buttons are also a big plus (large icons for pressing with fingers).


5) Did the site seem to facilitate people in a mobile environment (Think about the descriptions in the Visitor Considerations reading)? If "Yes", how?

The site does facilitate those in a mobile environment. It does this by changing a wep page full of information and options in a very clean and presentable form. I feel like the mobile site, in all reality, would server more as a stepping stone for people to learn about the services offered, and once the potential customer is hooked, they will turn to the desktop site to learn more and invest.

1) What was the worst site you came across during the 24 hour mobile browsing experience (list it's name and url)?

The worst site I came across during my 24 hours of browsing was www.scotch.io. This was dissapointing, as scotch.io is a wonderful site for developers to learn about lots of things, specifically about web design and frameworks. It's sad that their mobiel page has so many issues :'(


2) How did your best site differ from the worst in knowing who their target audience was?

www.typeforms.com seems to know their audience a tad bit better - if somseone is looking for an elegant solution to forms, the site advertizing it better well be elegant as well. On the other hand, www.scotch.io should know their audience, as they are advertizing to developers. They might, however, be expecting developers to be on a desktop where they would be writing the code they are learning about.


3) How did your best site differ from the worst in meeting the needs of their audience?

Like was stated in the question above, I think www.typeforms did a slightly better job here. This was lagely in part to their easy to navigate menu and organized content. Scotch.io, however, has design issues that make navigating the site difficult, if not impossoble, therefore impeding the needs of the user altogether.


4) How did your best site differ from the worst in organizing content to facilitate it being found?

This is where I feel www.typeforms.com excells the most - the content is well organized and easy to find what you need in an oder that makes sense. As stated above, scotch.io's navigation (although great on their desktop site) is a mess and is almost impossible to use.


5) How did your best site differ from the worst in facilitating visitors in a mobile environment?

I feel like www.typeforms.com really put time into making sure mobile users had a good experience and knew that a large part of their site visitors would be doing so on a mobiel device. Scotch.io, on the other hand, feels like all the effort went into the desktop site and they just kind of hoped it would work on mobile without actually putting any though into it.
